Understanding Mental Health

A friendly guide to what mental health means, why it matters, and simple ways to look after it as a university student.

Overview

What is mental health?

Mental health is how a person thinks, feels, and acts. It affects how they handle stress, relate to others, make decisions, and enjoy life. Just like physical health, mental health changes over time and can be strengthened with care and support.

Mood: feelings like calm, stress, sadness, joy, or worry.
Thoughts: the stories the mind tells about events and oneself.
Behaviours: choices and habits that can help or harm wellbeing.
Why it matters

Mental health and university life

University life can be exciting and intense. Coursework, finances, friendships, and big life changes can all add pressure. Paying attention to mental health helps with focus, motivation, relationships, sleep, and overall quality of life.

Performance: healthy routines support memory, energy, and planning.
Connection: feeling supported makes it easier to ask for help and set boundaries.
Resilience: small habits build capacity to handle setbacks and uncertainty.
Common experiences

What university students often face

Stress and burnout: back-to-back deadlines, part-time work, and poor sleep add up.
Anxiety: worry about grades, future, or social situations.
Low mood: homesickness, winter blues, or longer periods of feeling down.
Perfectionism: setting impossible standards and feeling like nothing is “good enough.”
Myths vs Facts

Tap a card to flip

Myth: “Asking for help is a sign of weakness.”
Fact: Reaching out is a skill. University students who seek support often recover faster.
Myth: “You can just snap out of it.”
Fact: Mental health shifts are real. Care, tools, and time make a difference.
Myth: “Only some people have mental health.”
Fact: Everyone has mental health—just like physical health.
Myth: “Talking about it makes it worse.”
Fact: Supportive conversations reduce shame and help people feel less alone.
When to seek help

Signals to pay attention to

Extra support is a smart choice if something feels off for more than a couple of weeks, or it’s affecting study, relationships, or safety.

Sleep shifts a lot, or energy is always low.
Worry or sadness is constant, or mood swings feel unmanageable.
Withdrawing from friends, skipping classes, or losing interest in usual activities.
Using alcohol or substances to cope.
Thoughts of harming oneself or not wanting to be here.

Self-care basics

Small habits that add up

Aim for a regular sleep window. Keep wake time steady if possible. Morning light, less caffeine late in the day, and a gentle wind-down routine all help.
Short walks, stretching, or gym sessions can ease stress and boost mood. Choose something doable and enjoyable.
Schedule low-pressure time with people who feel safe—coffee, study co-working, clubs, or call a friend.
Notice harsh thoughts and try a kinder alternative: “I’m learning,” “I can ask for help,” or “This doesn’t define me.”
Use tiny starts: 5–10 minutes, one paragraph, one email. Celebrate small wins to build momentum.
